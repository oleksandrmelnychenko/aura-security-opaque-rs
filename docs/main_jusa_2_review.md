The manuscript titled "Hybrid Post-Quantum Authentication from OPAQUE and ML-KEM-768: Construction, Formal Analysis, and a Reproducible Implementation" proposes a hybrid extension of OPAQUE in which ML-KEM-768 is combined with four Ristretto255 Diffie-Hellman terms within a transcript-bound key schedule. The construction preserves the KE1-KE2-KE3 interaction profile while increasing the handshake size to 2,715 bytes and reporting an end-to-end authentication time of 836.11 ms on the stated Linux/Xeon platform. The paper is timely and technically relevant, particularly because it attempts to connect protocol syntax, symbolic verification, Rust implementation evidence, and benchmark data. However, the current manuscript requires major revision before publication, mainly because several formal claims, deployment assumptions, and interoperability statements are stronger than the evidence presented can presently support.

1. The abstract on pages 1-2 is technically rich but not sufficiently focused. It reports the four-DH-plus-ML-KEM construction, the 836.11 ms end-to-end authentication time, the 492.30 microsecond ML-KEM round, and the 2,715-byte handshake. Still, it does not clearly identify the single primary scientific contribution. The abstract should distinguish more sharply between the protocol-design claim, the formal-modelling claim, and the implementation-performance claim. A shorter abstract, centred on the precise novelty of OPAQUE over classical OPAQUE, would give readers a clearer entry point.

2. The study objective on page 3 remains diffuse despite the useful list of research questions. The manuscript asks whether ML-KEM-768 can be incorporated without increasing the number of messages, how transcript binding should be handled, what evidence supports implementation-level claims, and whether Argon2id dominates latency. These are valid questions, but they should be unified under one explicit evaluative objective. The author should then map the ProVerif models, Tamarin surrogate model, Rust tests, and Criterion benchmarks directly to that objective. Moreover, keep no more than three main contributions in one manuscript.

3. The structure of the manuscript is comprehensive but overly segmented. By page 4, the reader has already encountered motivation, scope, six contributions, terminology, and multiple notation conventions before the main construction has begun. This fragmentation weakens the narrative because design choices, proof boundaries, and implementation evidence are interleaved before their evidentiary role is fully clear. The paper would benefit from reorganising the main text around a smaller set of claim-evidence pairs and moving secondary implementation detail to an appendix.

4. The manuscript is too long relative to its empirical scope. Although the paper spans protocol construction, symbolic modelling, Rust implementation, testing, and benchmarking, the empirical evaluation is still limited to a single implementation and one Linux/Xeon benchmark platform on pages 29-31. This imbalance makes the text feel more expansive than the evidence base can support. The author should reduce repeated explanatory material and reserve detailed implementation tables, traceability matrices, and secondary benchmark commentary for an appendix.

5. The Methods section should be compressed by removing repeated conceptual framing. Sections 4.1-4.5 repeatedly restate that the protocol preserves three messages, binds account context, and incorporates pkkem and ctkem into the transcript, while these points are already visible in Figure 1, Equations 15-17, and Table 6. The repetition slows the technical presentation and makes the construction appear more complex than it is. The author should retain the exact equations, message sizes, and validation rules, but remove redundant explanatory paragraphs that do not add new specification detail.

6. The Results section should distinguish core results from diagnostic or supporting results. The essential outcomes are the authentication-message expansion to 2,715 bytes, the added 379.45 microsecond KSF-free post-quantum cost, the 836.11 ms end-to-end authentication benchmark, and the reported symbolic-verification outcomes. By contrast, line-of-code counts, adapter-boundary checks, isolated primitive timings, and some state-lifecycle tests are useful diagnostics rather than central scientific findings. The author should reorganise Section 6 so that the main results appear first, followed by a clearly labelled subsection for implementation diagnostics.

7. The novelty claim in Section 2.6 on pages 8-9 needs stronger positioning against recent post-quantum PAKE work. The manuscript cites CHIC, UC/QROM-oriented schemes, PAKE combiners, and a 2025 CFRG-related hybrid PAKE draft, but the comparison remains largely descriptive. It should explain more precisely whether the contribution is a new protocol composition, an OPAQUE migration profile, an implementation artefact, or a formal-methods case study. A concise comparison of proof assumptions, transcript syntax, state migration, and deployability would make the contribution more persuasive.

8. The terminology for protocol roles is not sufficiently stable. On pages 9-12, the paper alternates between client/server, initiator/remote node, responder, and record-holding party, while the augmented PAKE setting depends critically on asymmetric storage assumptions. This matters because the database-compromise claim, OPRF-root-secret boundary, and authentication roles are easy to confuse if the labels shift. The author should choose one primary role vocabulary and use it consistently across the threat model, formal analysis, figures, and implementation discussion.

9. The treatment of the OPRF root secret σoprf is conceptually important but operationally underdeveloped. Pages 9-10 state that database-compromise resistance excludes compromise of σoprf, and page 19 confirms that exposure of this secret changes the problem into an offline dictionary search dominated by Argon2id. This is a central trust boundary, not a minor implementation detail. The manuscript should specify realistic assumptions for storage separation, access control, backup exclusion, rotation, and incident response for σoprf.

10. The mathematical notation on page 11 is precise but sometimes heavier than the surrounding explanation can support. Definitions 3.1-3.3 introduce KEM security, the hybrid aPAKE construction, HKDF-Extract pseudorandomness, and several advantage terms, but the corresponding security experiments are not fully specified. This gives the analysis a more theorem-like appearance than the manuscript later admits is justified. The author should add a brief recap of the compact notation and ensure that each advantage bound corresponds to an explicitly stated experiment or model assumption.

11. The Argon2id parameter choice on page 14 needs stronger justification. Equation 9 fixes a MODERATE profile with 256 MiB memory, three iterations, and one lane, and later benchmarks show that Argon2id dominates authentication latency. This parameter choice is therefore not incidental; it underpins the claim that ML-KEM overhead is practically negligible. The author should justify the setting against the intended deployment class and compare it with lighter and heavier KSF profiles.

12. The wire-format specification remains incomplete for independent implementation. Pages 13-16 give exact message sizes for KE1, KE2, and KE3, including the 1,273-byte and 1,377-byte hybrid wire formats, but they do not fully define canonical encodings, byte ordering, malformed-element rejection, or error categories. This is insufficient for interoperability in a cryptographic protocol. A normative wire-format subsection should specify serialisation boundaries and validation rules without requiring readers to inspect the Rust code.

13. The residual risk after σoprf compromise is not quantified adequately. Page 19 correctly explains that compromise of σoprf allows each password guess to be checked via the OPRF path, then via Argon2id. Yet the analysis stops at the expression Coffline ≈ |D| · Tguess and does not consider parallel hardware, memory-bandwidth limits, or realistic password distributions. A practical breach analysis would make the security boundary much more useful to system operators.

14. The formal security claims on pages 19-20 are stronger in presentation than in proof support. Claims 5.1 and 5.2 use theorem-like language for scoped forward secrecy and mutual authentication, but the supporting material consists mainly of proof sketches and symbolic model interpretations. This creates tension with the later acknowledgement that the models do not constitute a machine-checked proof of the implementation. The author should either supply formal games and explicit reductions or soften the claims into scoped security arguments.

15. The side-channel discussion on page 22 is too brief for a paper that presents a reference cryptographic implementation. The manuscript states that MAC comparisons are constant-time and that Ristretto255 and ML-KEM components are constant-time-oriented, while also admitting that microarchitectural leakage is outside the symbolic models. Critical operations such as Argon2id, ML-KEM decapsulation, parsing failures, and error handling may still leak timing information. The author should add leakage-oriented testing or state more prominently that side-channel resistance is not part of the validated claim set.

16. Table 7 on page 22 compresses too much evidence into one difficult matrix. It combines ProVerif outcomes, Tamarin lemma steps, and Rust test counts, including 5/5 ProVerif queries, 8/8 Tamarin lemmas, and 157 passing tests. These categories do not have equivalent evidentiary meaning, yet the layout risks making them appear directly comparable. The table should be split or heavily annotated to clearly separate symbolic secrecy, symbolic authentication, implementation regression tests, and property tests.

17. The split ProVerif modelling strategy on page 23 needs deeper justification. The manuscript states that secrecy and authentication were analysed in separate models because a single saturated model failed to converge stably across all queries. This is understandable, but it leaves open whether interactions between secrecy failures, compromise events, and correspondence assertions were abstracted away. The author should document the abstractions in each ProVerif file and explain why the two models remain mutually consistent.

18. The Tamarin abstraction on pages 23-24 is too coarse to support some of the paper's stronger interpretations. The model replaces the concrete 4DH construction with an abstract secret ξ4dh = h(<skC, skS, eC, eS>), thereby avoiding the algebraic structure of the Ristretto255 operations. This is a major limitation because the fourth DH term is part of the claimed design rationale. The manuscript should either model the group operations more faithfully or substantially reduce the evidentiary weight assigned to the Tamarin result.

19. The implementation evidence is promising but not sufficiently auditable from the paper alone. Page 28 reports 157 passing tests and one ignored slow regression, while Tables 9 and 10 describe workspace size and specification-to-implementation traceability. A test count alone cannot establish adequate protocol coverage. The manuscript should include an artefact appendix with commit hash, dependency lockfile status, exact test commands, benchmark commands, and expected outputs.

20. The benchmark platform limits the generality of the performance claims. Page 29 states that testing was performed on a Linux server with two Intel Xeon E5-2650 v4 processors, 24 physical cores, 48 hardware threads, and Rust 1.93.1. That platform does not represent mobile clients, embedded devices, browsers, or edge deployments, where a 256 MiB Argon2id profile may be prohibitive. The author should either add measurements on consumer and constrained devices or restrict the deployment claims to server-class environments.

21. The manuscript would benefit from a stronger editorial pass. Around pages 29-31, the presentation becomes table-heavy, with dense benchmark tables and substantial whitespace around floating elements. This interrupts the reading flow at precisely the point where the performance interpretation should be clearest. The author should improve table placement, reduce repetitive explanatory prose, and enforce consistent British-English style and hyphenation throughout.

22. Table 12 on page 31 is potentially confusing despite the explanatory paragraph below it. It reports KE3 generation at 1.101 s, while the full end-to-end authentication cycle is reported at 836.11 ms, which can appear contradictory unless one notes that the measurements are separate Criterion workloads. The current layout invites readers to treat the rows as additive phase components. The benchmark section should more explicitly separate isolated phase measurements from holistic protocol-cycle measurements.

23. The Conclusion on page 36 restates the main metrics but does not sufficiently prioritise the remaining blockers. It notes the 836.11 ms end-to-end authentication time, the dominance of Argon2id, the 8/8 Tamarin lemmas, the 5/5 ProVerif queries, and the limitation that dictionary resistance excludes compromise of σoprf. These are important, but the Conclusion should more decisively separate validated results from open problems. The final paragraph should foreground the need for a computational combiner proof, RFC 9807 alignment, broader hardware benchmarking, and operational treatment of σoprf.

24. Moreover, the Conclusion should be separated into three clearly identifiable parts. First, it should state the numerical results: 2,715-byte handshake size, 2,272-byte post-quantum message expansion, 836.11 ms end-to-end authentication time, and the sub-millisecond ML-KEM and 4DH components reported in Tables 11 and 12. Second, it should briefly state the limitations, including the σoprf trust boundary, the surrogate Tamarin 4DH abstraction, the split ProVerif modelling strategy, the single-platform benchmark, and the lack of full RFC 9807 conformance. Third, it should identify future work, especially a computational combiner proof, broader hardware evaluation, side-channel analysis, and a conformance-oriented profile for standard OPAQUE deployment.

25. The bibliography on pages 38-41 requires more careful handling of provisional and forward-looking sources. The manuscript cites stable standards such as FIPS 203 and RFC 9807 alongside Internet-Drafts, white papers, and 2025-2026 materials. In a fast-moving post-quantum standardisation context, these source types should not be presented with equal authority. The reference list and related-work discussion should explicitly distinguish final standards, peer-reviewed papers, preprints, Internet-Drafts, and technical white papers.

26. The manuscript should redistribute several citations from the technical and discussion sections into the Introduction or Related Work. For example, sources connected to Argon2id, HKDF, Criterion benchmarking, RFC 9807, FIPS 203, Ristretto255, hybrid TLS, PQXDH, and post-quantum PAKE should help motivate the design problem before the protocol is specified. The current placement makes some references function as after-the-fact justification rather than as evidence of a pre-existing research gap. The author should ensure that the requested 30+ references are not merely present in the manuscript but actively used to position the contribution in Sections 1 and 2.

27. Some older references should be refreshed or contextualised with newer standards and post-quantum migration literature. Since the manuscript relies on ML-KEM-768, FIPS 203 should be treated as the primary standard reference rather than being overshadowed by older Kyber-era descriptions. Similarly, the relationship to OPAQUE should be anchored in RFC 9807, especially because the manuscript later admits that the implementation is OPAQUE-like rather than fully RFC-conformant. Older PAKE and password-authentication references remain valuable, but they should be balanced with current standardisation sources and recent hybrid PAKE work.

28. The self-citations currently placed late in the manuscript should be moved or reframed within the Introduction if they are intended to justify the application context. References [35]-[37] appear on page 34 in connection with audit-heavy cyber-physical deployments, but their relevance to post-quantum password authentication is not established early enough. If these works are important for motivating secure authentication in UAVs, SCADA systems, or cyber-physical inspection systems, the Introduction should explicitly explain that link. Otherwise, the citations risk appearing opportunistic and should be reduced or moved to a narrower application-context paragraph.

29. The manuscript needs a production-level figure and caption audit before journal submission. Figures 1-5 contain dense labels, equations, and visual elements that may not remain legible after typesetting, while several captions do not fully define symbols, abbreviations, benchmark context, or plotted variables. The author should redraw the protocol and benchmark figures as clean vector artwork, simplify internal labels, and prepare self-contained captions in line with journal production requirements.

30. The manuscript should be prepared more carefully as a complete submission package, not only as a technical PDF. The current ending includes an acknowledgement of OpenAI Codex use on page 37. Yet, Elsevier requires generative-AI use in manuscript preparation to be declared in a dedicated section before the references, with the tool name, purpose of use, and author responsibility clearly stated. The same production pass should also ensure that the artefact repository, tests, formal models, and benchmark data are cited or linked via an appropriate data/software availability statement, as the journal encourages the deposition, citation, and linking of research data and related materials. The author should therefore separate acknowledgements, AI-use declaration, data/software availability, funding, and CRediT-style contribution statements so that the article is ready for editorial processing rather than only technical review.

In summary, the manuscript presents a relevant and technically ambitious contribution to post-quantum password authentication. Its strongest features are the explicit 4DH + ML-KEM-768 key schedule, the transcript binding of pkkem, ctkem, and acct, the reproducible Rust artefact, and the effort to align symbolic verification with implementation tests and benchmarks. Nevertheless, the paper requires major revision because the formal security claims, the AND-style combiner interpretation, the σoprf trust boundary, and the deployment claims are not yet supported at the level needed for a practical cryptographic protocol.
